Definite Integrals



What's the difference?

You've learned about the indefinite integral and now there's a definite integral, what's different? Well the definite integral is simply an integral over an interval instead of the entire fucntion, and it looks something like this ∫15f(x)dxThe new numbers at the end of the integral indicate the interval that it's evaluating. In this case that would be the interval from x=1 to x=5. There's also some special notation involved after you've solved for the antiderivative of the function to indicate which interval the should be solved for. f(x)|15To completely solve the definite integral simply substitute the upper number as your variable and solve, repeat for the lower number, then subtract the lower result from the upper result.


Now what does it mean?

Just like the indefinite integral, the definite integral also represents the area under a curve. However, this time we can actually find a value for that area instead of just getting a function that describes the area under the curve. Now we can actually find that area. Don't forget though, if a curve ends up going below the x-axis it will have negative area! That means you might get values that are smaller than what you expect depending on the situation. If you don't want this then you'll have to calculate each area above the x-axis and each area below the x-axis separately and then combine them.
