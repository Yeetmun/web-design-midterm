General Solving Methods



What's this all about?

There are many different integral problems that need different methods to be solved. Of course, with computers now it is easy to solve them but it's useful to know how to do it by hand too. There are many different basic rules for integrating that can make things a lot easier when trying to solve integrals.


Constant Rule

A simple rule that states: The indefinite integral for any constant k is simply k times the variable of integration. That is: ∫kdx=kx


Power Rule

A rule that is almost always useful since exponents are so common in integrals. It states that: The definite integral of any variable rasied to a power n where n does not equal 1 is equal to the variable raised to the n+1th power over n+1. That is: ∫xndx=xn+1n+1+C


Constant Multiple Rule

In a derivative, any constants being multiplied with a variable can be brought out and multiplied after solving the derivative. Similarly, any constants being multiplied with a variable or function in an integral can be brought outside of the integral. That is: ∫k⋅f(x)dx=k∫f(x)dx


Sum or Difference Rule

If there is a sum or difference in an integral, it is equal to the sum or difference of the integrals of each term individually. That is: ∫[f(x)+g(x)]dx=∫f(x)dx+∫g(x)dx

These are the most common and basic methods available to solve integrals. However, they most certainly aren't the only methods available. There are plenty of other methods available for solving more complex integrals. Two of such methods, u-substitution and integration by parts, are available on other pages of this site to learn from.
