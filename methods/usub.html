Solving Integrals with U-Substitution


" _-substitution intro | AP Calculus AB | Khan Academy" by Khan Academy is licensed under CC BY-NC-SA


Intro

U-substitution, also called the Reverse Chain Rule or simply Integration by Substitution, is a method to find the integral for certain integrals that may prove tricky to solve with other methods. The most important step in u-substitution is determining if it's even possible to do in the first place. For this method to work your integral must be in the form of ∫f(g(x))⋅g'(x)dx


Method

Now that you've determined u-substitution is possible, we can perform the substitution part. First, substitute g(x) with u. Then, substitute g'(x)dx with du like this ∫f(g(x))⋅g'(x)dx→∫f(u)du Now you can integrate like normal. After that, simply replace the original terms to arrive at your answer.


Example

Let's go through an example, say we have the following integral ∫cos(x2)2xdx We can see that the derivative of the function inside of the cosine is equal to the term 2x on the outside. That means we can use u-substition. ∫cos(x2)2xdx→∫cos(u)du Now we can solve the integral like normal ∫cos(u)du=sin(u)+C And finally we put back our u=x2 back in to get our answer of sin(x2)+C


Addendum

Sometimes your integrals won't work out all nice like that. That's no problem though! Oftentimes you can rearrange terms or apply different rules of integration in order to get the form you want and then solve for u-substition. Just make sure you account for all the numbers and don't forget anything!
